Cultural & Diversity Contexts

Supporting First Nations, Immigrant & Refugee Families

Understanding the Context

What Are Cultural & Diversity Contexts?

These contexts denote the cultural identities, languages, customs, and experiences of the families. They comprise the following:

  • First Nations families
  • Culturally and linguistically diverse (CALD) families
  • Refugee and asylum-seeker families
  • Migrant families
  • Multilingual households
  • Families preserving strong cultural traditions

Grasping the concept of culture is a prerequisite for applying respectful, inclusive, and anti-bias practices in early childhood settings.

Why This Matters in Early Childhood Education

The process of cultural identity and connection helps children discover who they are. Culturally safe environments:

  • multiply belonging
  • encourage emotional wellbeing
  • lead to better learning
  • determine the identity and pride
  • help avoid discrimination and bias

It is the teachers' duty to build the kind of settings where diversity is not only accepted but also appreciated.

Relevant Sociological & Educational Theories

• Anti-bias Approach (Derman-Sparks)
Fairness, cultural identity, and bias are the main points of the approach.

• Bronfenbrenner's Ecological Systems Theory
The child's whole environment is affected by culture, layer by layer.

• Funds of Knowledge Theory
Cultural knowledge from families ought to be treated as a treasure by teachers.

• Critical Race Theory
Talks about the existence of systemic inequalities and the need for cultural safety among minorities.

Contemporary Research

  • Culturally responsive practices lead to improvement in children's overall wellbeing, confidence, and motivation to learn (DEEWR, 2022).
  • The curriculum that is well grounded in Country, language, and culture offers First Nations children better outcomes (AIHW, 2023).
  • Trauma-informed, culturally safe environments are beneficial for refugee children (Refugee Council, 2021).
  • Multilingualism is a factor in the development of both brain and identity (Research Australia, 2020).

Diversity and Evolving Nature

The cultural diversity in Australia is characterized by:

  • More than 200 languages spoken
  • 1 in 3 people in Australia are foreigners
  • A very large First Nations cultural diversity (including more than 250 language groups)
  • An increasing number of multilingual families
  • Arrival of refugees from war-torn countries such as Afghanistan, Syria, Myanmar, and Sudan

With the rapid growth of cultural diversity, the need to practice inclusivity in the early childhood sector becomes more pressing.

Impact on Children and Families

Impact on Children

  • The strong cultural identity helps in the child's self-esteem and feeling of belonging
  • Cultural mismatch may lead to confusion or even to the child's feeling of shame
  • Racism or exclusion may affect the child's wellbeing adversely
  • Children who are multilingual may need more time to switch from one language to another
  • Children who are refugees might go through trauma, grief, or loss issues

Impact on Families

  • The process of coping with new systems may be very difficult
  • Language problems hinder communication
  • There are different cultural expectations about the education of the children
  • Parents may be facing racism or discrimination as well
  • Trauma caused by migration or displacement can be felt by the whole family

Implications for Early Childhood Services

Educators must:

  • Implement culturally responsive pedagogy
  • Engage in respectful collaboration with families
  • Be careful not to make presumptions about cultural practices
  • Representation in books, resources, and play should be guaranteed
  • Home languages should be supported
  • Cross-cultural relationships should be built

Social Policy & Australian Responses

Important Statistics

  • 3% of the population identifies as Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander (ABS).
  • Over 300 languages are spoken in Australian homes.
  • 1 in 5 children speak a language other than English at home.
  • Refugee children experience higher emotional distress than non-refugee children (AIHW).

Key Policies

  • Closing the Gap
  • Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Child Placement Principle
  • Multicultural Australia Policy
  • Racism. It Stops With Me Campaign
  • NQS – Cultural Competence Standard
  • EYLF V2.0 – Belonging, Being & Becoming

How Policies Shape Early Childhood Practice

  • Embed First Nations perspectives
  • Provide culturally safe communication
  • Build relationships based on respect
  • Promote anti-bias learning
  • Ensure equitable access to services

Strategies for Practice

Five Proven Teaching Techniques

Strategy 1: Daily Integration of First Nations Perspectives

Make use of Acknowledgment of Country, stories, language, art, and nature in a respectful and authentic manner and children will benefit from this.

Strategy 2: Home Languages Support

Bilingual books, visual aids, interpreters, and speaking home languages at home should be used and promoted.

Strategy 3: Anti-Bias Curriculum

Stereotypes should be challenged, diversity celebrated, and fairness and respect promoted.

Strategy 4: Cultural Partnerships with Families

Acquire information from families about their traditions, values, and celebrations; involve them in the program.

Strategy 5: Trauma-Informed Practice for Refugee Families

Provide calm environments, predictable routines, and gentle communication.
