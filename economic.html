Economic Contexts

Supporting Families Experiencing Poverty, Housing Stress & Financial Hardship

Understanding the Context

What Are Economic Contexts?

Economic contexts are the different financial and material methods non-privileged people face, which determine a family's capability to provide for their basic needs. Such conditions involve poverty, lack of stable housing, temporary jobs, and a variety of other factors that increase living costs and limit people's access to resources.

Why This Matters in Early Childhood Education

Economic inequality is one of the most significant predictors of a child's developmental and educational outcomes. Therefore, educators should not see the family as the sole factor that influences children's experiences but rather the structural factors.

Relevant Sociological Theories

• Bronfenbrenner's Ecological Systems Theory
Presents the idea that poverty cannot be an individual problem but a systemic one that impacts a child through the family, school, and parent's employment levels.

• Social Determinants of Health
Economic stability, housing, access to food, and healthcare are major factors influencing children's wellbeing (AIHW, 2023).

• Structural Inequality Theory
Proclaims that poverty is determined by larger social structures such as labour markets, welfare policies, and economic systems not individual failure.

Recent Studies

The Australian Early Development Census (AEDC) has reported that children who come from financially disadvantaged families are more likely to suffer from developmental problems in the areas of language, social skills and emotional health.

Davidson and his colleagues have found that housing instability is a factor that negatively affects the quality of life of the individuals involved as it impacts their stress, sleep, health and eventually their participation in the early learning processes.

OECD has concluded that early intervention and quality education are the ways to protect children from the effects of poverty to a very great extent.

Variety and Transforming Aspect

Economic pressure in Australia is increasingly impacting the following groups:

  • One-parent families
  • Migrant and refugee families
  • Families under pressure due to high rent
  • Indigenous people suffering from systemic inequalities
  • Families dealing with rising prices and cost of living

Economic disadvantage has become a collective problem that ever-wider parts of society have to face, thus, it is a factor influencing the development of today's children.

Impact on Children and Families

Impact on Children's Development

The negative effects brought about by poverty can impact the following areas:

  • Physical health: Poor diet, constant stress, and unavailability of medical services.
  • Cognitive development: Limitations on language exposure, and lack of access to educational resources.
  • Social-emotional well-being: Anxiety, shame, and low self-esteem due to the realization of differences.
  • Routine & learning: Frequent absences, tiredness, and inability to focus.

Impact on Family Relationships

  • Parents may experience feelings of guilt, stress, or embarrassment.
  • The economic crisis can make parents less emotionally present.
  • Unstable housing conditions can break family ties and cause disruptions in family routine.
  • Living in environments that are either too small or not secure raises the level of stress for every family member.

Implications for Early Childhood Services

Teachers will be required to be:

  • Non-judgmental
  • Strength-based
  • Flexible with expectations
  • Aware of barriers to participation
  • Skilled in linking families to support services

Social Policy and Australian Responses

Relevant Data & Statistics

  • According to ACOSS, 1 in 6 children in Australia is living in poverty (2022).
  • Income stress affects 40% of households with single mothers.
  • In the past ten years, housing insecurity has escalated by 14%.
  • The AEDC reveals that children from low-income families have a 100% higher chance of being developmentally vulnerable.

Key National and State Policies

  • Australian Early Development Census (AEDC)
    Economic disadvantage is associated with monitoring of the stratification of children regarding their vulnerabilities.
  • Child Care Subsidy (CCS)
    Low-income families have affordable access to early learning opportunities.
  • National Housing and Homelessness Agreement (NHHA)
    It is all about the government funding for crisis accommodation and long-term housing strategy.
  • Centrelink Family Tax Benefits & Parenting Payments
    Financial aid intended for distressed families.
  • Closing the Gap Policy
    Socioeconomic differences between First Nations families and the rest of the population are reduced.

Influence on Early Childhood Practice

It is the policies that determine:

  • The amount of financial support for learning and care
  • How easy it is to access support services
  • Which children are considered as needing support the most
  • Reporting obligations
  • The amount of money allocated for inclusive programs

Strategies for Practice

Five Evidence-Based Strategies for Educators

Strategy 1: Build Strong, Respectful Relationships

A non-judgmental, empathetic approach should be applied. Instead of pointing out family weaknesses, strengths should be confirmed. A safe, welcoming place that lessens stigma should be created.

Strategy 2: Provide Predictable Routines and Emotional Security

Children who are going through tough times with money usually have chaotic domestic environments. The teachers can:

  • Prepare daily routines that are the same every day
  • Implement visual timetables
  • Allow to make choices to develop independence
Strategy 3: Embed Strength-Based Storytelling and Representation

Select books and stories that present different kinds of families in a positive light. This makes the feeling of shame less and belonging more.

Strategy 4: Support Access to Material Resources

Link families to:

  • Food banks
  • Help for housing
  • Information on subsidies
  • Local playgroups
  • Clothing libraries
  • Financial counselling services
Strategy 5: Collaborate with Specialists and Community Services

Engage with:

  • Social workers
  • Family support workers
  • Housing case managers
  • Early intervention specialists

This provides comprehensive, wrap-around support.
