Social Contexts

Supporting Children Experiencing Social/Geographical Isolation, Divorce & Out-of-Home Care

Understanding the Context

What Are Social Contexts?

When we talk about social contexts, we talk about the relationships, networks, and social environments the families are in. These could be:

  • divorce or separation
  • shared parenting
  • social or geographic isolation
  • out-of-home care (OOHC)
  • kinship care
  • newly blended families
  • single-parent households
  • family conflict

All these contexts influence children's social worlds, relationships, belonging, and emotional safety.

Why This Matters in Early Childhood Education

Children going through social disruption or relational change nearly always require:

  • stability
  • predictable routines
  • strong relationships with educators
  • emotional support
  • opportunities to express feelings safely

Teachers have to learn about social contexts as factors that influence behavioural changes, attachment and learning.

Relevant Sociological & Developmental Theories

• Attachment Theory (Bowlby)
Children are often dependent on stable relationships. Changes like separation or out-of-home care (OOHC) can disturb attachment and regulation of emotions.

• Bronfenbrenner's Ecological Systems Theory
The separation of family, being socially isolated and the arrangements for care are issues that belong to the microsystem and the mesosystem thus having an impact on the entire development.

• Resilience Theory
Identifies the role of adults who support the child, the establishment of regular routines and the giving of social attention as the protective measures.

Contemporary Research

  • Children suffering from parental separation may reveal emotional distress, anxiety, or alterations in their behaviour (AIFS, 2022).
  • Children in OOHC are more likely to have a history of trauma, need predictable care and stable relationships (AIHW, 2023).
  • During early childhood, social isolation raises the risks of loneliness, delayed communication and acquisition of social skills becoming less (Hancock et al., 2021).
  • The presence of a consistent educator relationship helps to build resilience and promote the well-being of children going through a family transition (Center on the Developing Child, 2021).

Diversity & Evolving Nature

Social contexts differ from one family to another:

  • First Nations families commonly use kinship systems in their daily lives
  • Refugee families might go through a period of isolation and disconnection from the community
  • CALD families may practice different cultural norms regarding separation
  • Geographic isolation is a characteristic of rural families that impacts their access to services
  • Australia sees an increase in divorce and blended families

Children of today are growing up in family structures that are more and more diverse and complex.

Impact on Children and Families

Impact on Children's Development

• Emotional wellbeing:
Children can feel a range of emotions, including sadness, confusion, separation anxiety, anger, insecurity, or hypervigilance.

• Social development:
Instability can make children have a hard time in trusting others or making friends.

• Behaviour:
Children may show different behaviours like regressions, withdrawal, aggression, clinginess, and sleep disruptions.

• Cognitive development:
Stress is like a blocker for focus, memory, problem-solving, and learning engagement which are all areas of cognitive development that get affected negatively.

Impact on Family Relationships

  • Parents' stress managing separation or shared custody
  • Hard communication
  • Different parenting styles in each household
  • Single parents having to take on more duties
  • Cutting off of extended family networks
  • For OOHC: mourning, losing, cultural uprooting, identity distress

Implications for Early Childhood Services

Educators for the future should:

  • not make family structure assumptions
  • talk diplomatically and like professionals
  • keep information about families confidential
  • offer and support co-regulation and emotional safety
  • make all family types feel they belong through the environment
  • and last but not least, be understanding with trauma and changes in behavior.

Social Policy & Australian Responses

Australian Statistics

  • 1 in 4 Australian children go through parental separation.
  • Over 46,000 kids are staying in OOHC (AIHW, 2023).
  • Social isolation impacts refugees, low-income families, and single-parent households the most.

Relevant Policies

  • Child Safe Standards
    Child protection in OOHC and families at risk.
  • National Framework for Protecting Australia's Children
    Helps safety, wellbeing, and rights for children in care.
  • Family Law Act (Shared Parental Responsibility)
    Children's relationships and stability during separation are supported.
  • OOHC Standards (State-based)
    The needs of children in care are met from all aspects, including culture, emotion, and education.
  • NQS & ACECQA
    Quality standards that are primarily focused on relationships, partnerships, and the well-being of children.

How Policies Influence Practice

Policies give directions to and set the framework for educators to:

  • safeguard and secure environments
  • apply trauma-informed methods
  • give top priority to wellness
  • work together with family support agencies
  • obey court orders and custody agreements
  • provide cultural safety (especially for First Nations children)

Strategies for Practice

Five Evidence-Based Strategies

Strategy 1: Build Predictable, Secure Relationships
  • Utilize good interactions that are warm and consistence.
  • Give co-regulation, emotional naming, and comfort.
  • Create a safe base for children going through instability.
Strategy 2: Use Trauma-Informed Practice
  • Treat behaviour as a form of communication.
  • Do not punish or shame.
  • Provide quiet spaces, sensory tools, and gentle guidance.
Strategy 3: Strengthen Belonging Through Inclusive Environments
  • Show multicultural family photos and representations.
  • Include different family structures in books and play.
  • Make it clear that no child is "different" because of their home situation.
Strategy 4: Provide Emotional Literacy Opportunities

Put storybooks, puppets, role play and feelings cards into use. Help children to identify emotions and to request assistance.

Strategy 5: Collaborate with Family Support Professionals

Engage with:

  • Social workers
  • OOHC case managers
  • Counsellors
  • Family law services
  • Cultural support workers

This way, coordinated, wrap-around care is ensured.

How These Resources Support Learning

These resources support learning in many ways such as:

  • Children being ready for changes in real life.
  • Emotional literacy served by these resources.
  • Families composed of different kinds being accepted as normal.
  • Children being taught through narratives that they may experience, thus their emotions not being associated with shame...
  • Quite the opposite, children being helped to show what they feel.
  • Resiliency being created through predictable stories.
  • Empathy and understanding being the consequences.
