Health & Wellbeing Contexts

Supporting Families Affected by Mental Illness, Substance Use, Trauma, Grief & Abuse

Understanding the Context

What Are Health & Wellbeing Contexts?

Health and wellbeing contexts include the physical, emotional, and psychological conditions that shape how children and families function. These may include:

  • parental or child mental illness (e.g., anxiety, depression, PTSD)
  • family violence and unsafe home environments
  • substance use
  • chronic illness (e.g., asthma, diabetes)
  • grief and loss
  • trauma (acute, developmental, or complex)

These experiences can impact children's emotional stability, daily routines, safety, learning, and behaviour.

Why This Matters in Early Childhood Education

The early years are a sensitive period for brain development. Chronic stress or exposure to trauma affects how children:

  • regulate emotions
  • process new information
  • form relationships
  • trust adults
  • develop confidence and resilience

Early childhood settings become protective environments, especially when home life is unstable. Educators therefore play a critical role in:

  • co-regulation
  • emotional safety
  • predictable routines
  • identifying early signs of distress
  • supporting families without judgement
  • making referrals to services

Relevant Sociological & Developmental Theories

• Trauma Theory (Bruce Perry, Bessel van der Kolk)
The trauma experienced reconfigures the whole brain structure of the child as it is developing. Children can act out through very strong feelings, become uncommunicative, very active in their surroundings, or behave in a manner that is not constant.

• Attachment Theory (Bowlby & Ainsworth)
A secure emotional bond is a good predictor of psychological health. Family violence, parental mental disorders, and inconsistent caregiving are some of the factors that can break attachment networks.

• Social Determinants of Health
The mental health of the family, stability of housing, and access to services determine children's health indirectly through the family dynamics.

• Ecological Systems Theory (Bronfenbrenner)
Health and wellbeing issues in the microsystem (home) ripple outward, affecting learning and behavior in all other settings.

• Resilience Theory
The presence of one stable, supportive adult greatly reduces the negative effects of trauma and stress.

Contemporary Research

  • Toxic stress harms cognitive functioning, memory, and emotional regulation (Center on the Developing Child, 2020).
  • Children exposed to family violence are at higher risk for anxiety, behavioural challenges, and developmental delays (AIFS, 2022).
  • Parental mental illness affects approximately 23% of children in Australia (AIHW, 2023).
  • Early childhood services that implement trauma-informed practices improve behaviour, wellbeing, and engagement (Be You, 2023).
  • Grief in young children is often expressed through behaviour rather than words (ECA, 2021).

Diversity & Evolving Nature

Different communities experience health and wellbeing contexts differently:

  • First Nations families may experience intergenerational trauma due to colonisation and cultural displacement.
  • Refugee families may have trauma histories related to war, loss, and migration.
  • CALD families may face stigma around mental health and may not access services easily.
  • Rural families may have limited access to health professionals.
  • Same-sex or blended families may experience unique social stressors and discrimination.

Health and wellbeing challenges are increasingly recognised, with services improving mental health support for young children and families.

Impact on Children and Families

Impact on Children's Development

• Emotional Development
Children may show:

  • heightened anxiety
  • fearfulness
  • sadness
  • irritability
  • emotional outbursts
  • difficulties with self-regulation

• Social Development
Children might:

  • struggle with trust
  • avoid peers
  • show clinginess
  • become aggressive or withdrawn

• Behaviour & Learning

  • difficulty concentrating
  • lower persistence with tasks
  • sensory overload
  • regression in skills (sleep, toileting, language)

• Physical Wellbeing
Stress-related physical symptoms may include headaches, stomach aches, or fatigue.

Impact on Family Relationships

Families may experience:

  • increased conflict or tension
  • inconsistent responses to children
  • reduced parental capacity
  • emotional unavailability
  • grief impacting communication
  • secrecy or shame around family issues
  • disruptions to routines and safety

Implications for Early Childhood Services

Educators must:

  • read behaviour as communication
  • provide emotional co-regulation
  • maintain confidentiality
  • avoid making assumptions
  • ensure cultural sensitivity
  • prioritise safety and wellbeing
  • provide consistent, predictable routines
  • collaborate with wellbeing professionals
  • support children's rights and agency

Social Policy & Australian Responses

Key Australian Statistics

  • 1 in 5 adults experiences mental illness in any given year (AIHW).
  • 1 in 4 children has witnessed family violence.
  • Family violence is the leading cause of homelessness for women and children.
  • Children exposed to trauma are significantly more likely to show developmental vulnerability.

Relevant Policies

  • MARAM Framework (Victoria)
    Guides safe and consistent responses to family violence.
  • National Mental Health Strategy
    Supports mental health promotion, prevention, and early intervention.
  • Child Safe Standards
    Ensure children's safety, participation, and wellbeing.
  • National Framework for Protecting Australia's Children
    Protects vulnerable children and supports early intervention.
  • Be You Initiative (Beyond Blue + Early Childhood Australia)
    Provides mental health resources for early childhood services.
  • Mandatory Reporting Requirements (State-Based)
    Educators must report serious concerns around harm or risk.

Influence on Early Childhood Practice

Policies require educators to:

  • maintain safe environments
  • create wellbeing-focused programs
  • follow reporting obligations
  • collaborate with health and support services
  • embed trauma-informed practices
  • document concerns sensitively and professionally

Strategies for Practice

Five Evidence-Based Strategies

Strategy 1: Trauma-Informed Practice
  • Use calm, predictable routines.
  • Offer quiet spaces and sensory tools.
  • Avoid sudden changes or loud transitions.
  • Respond to behaviour with empathy, not punishment.
Strategy 2: Emotion Coaching & Co-Regulation
  • Label children's emotions gently.
  • Model coping strategies (breathing, stretching, grounding).
  • Acknowledge feelings before guiding behaviour.
  • Provide warm, consistent reassurance.
Strategy 3: Visual Routines & Predictability

Use visual timetables, first–then boards, calm-down areas. Predictable structure helps children feel safe when home is unpredictable.

Strategy 4: Strength-Based Partnership With Families
  • Avoid judgement about mental illness or family violence.
  • Offer resources privately and respectfully.
  • Communicate in culturally sensitive ways.
Strategy 5: Link Families to Support Services

Work with:

  • psychologists
  • counsellors
  • mental health nurses
  • family violence support workers
  • social workers
  • child protection
  • local wellbeing hubs

Referral pathways help families access specialised help sooner.
