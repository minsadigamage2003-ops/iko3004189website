Crisis & Emergency Contexts

Responding to Climate Events, Natural Disasters, Family Violence & Displacement

Understanding the Context

What Are Crisis & Emergency Contexts?

Crisis and emergency contexts refer to sudden or ongoing events that cause significant stress, disruption, or danger to children and families. These can include:

  • natural disasters (floods, bushfires, storms)
  • climate emergencies
  • pandemics or health outbreaks
  • sudden family violence crises
  • homelessness or displacement
  • emergency relocations
  • community-wide emergencies
  • parental incarceration

These situations can be frightening, unpredictable, and traumatic for young children, who rely on adults to maintain safety and stability.

Why This Matters in Early Childhood Education

Crisis and emergency situations:

  • disrupt children's routines
  • cause fear, anxiety, confusion, and insecurity
  • lead to sudden changes in caregivers, housing, or community
  • increase vulnerability to emotional and behavioural challenges
  • escalate stress for families

Early childhood services play a protective role by:

  • offering stability
  • ensuring safety
  • providing calm, consistent routines
  • supporting emotional recovery
  • connecting families to emergency services
  • promoting community resilience

Relevant Theories

• Trauma Theory (Perry, van der Kolk)
Exposure to crisis activates stress responses. Children show big emotions, behavioural shifts, hypervigilance, or withdrawal.

• Ecological Systems Theory (Bronfenbrenner)
Crisis affects every layer of the child's system — home, community, school, services, and environment.

• Resilience Theory
Children recover better when they have stable, supportive adults and predictable environments.

• Attachment Theory
In emergencies, children seek secure relationships. Educators become a secondary secure base.

Contemporary Research

  • Children who experience natural disasters have heightened emotional distress, anxiety, and fear (Australian Red Cross, 2022).
  • Crisis-related displacement increases risks of developmental delays and behavioural challenges (AIHW, 2023).
  • Community emergencies (bushfires, floods) often lead to trauma symptoms, sleep disturbances, and separation anxiety in young children (AIFS, 2021).
  • Predictable routines and strong educator relationships significantly reduce the negative effects of crisis exposure (Center on the Developing Child, 2020).

Diversity & Evolving Nature

Different communities experience emergencies differently:

  • First Nations families face increased climate-related risks in remote areas.
  • Rural and regional communities may experience more extreme weather events.
  • Refugee families may re-experience trauma triggered by crisis.
  • Families experiencing poverty recover more slowly due to limited resources.
  • CALD families may need language support during emergency alerts.

Climate change has increased the frequency and intensity of natural disasters in Australia, meaning emergency preparedness is essential.

Impact on Children and Families

Impact on Children

Emotional:

  • fear, worry, panic
  • nightmares
  • separation anxiety
  • clinginess
  • anger or sadness

Behavioural:

  • regression
  • hyperactivity
  • withdrawal
  • difficulty managing emotions

Learning:

  • reduced focus
  • low persistence
  • increased sensitivity to noise or transitions

Impact on Families

  • shock, fear, grief, trauma
  • sudden homelessness or relocation
  • loss of employment or financial stress
  • strained relationships
  • uncertainty about safety
  • disrupted routines and support networks

Implications for Early Childhood Services

Educators must:

  • create calm, predictable routines
  • offer emotional safety and reassurance
  • ensure emergency procedures are known and practiced
  • maintain trauma-informed, sensitive communication
  • collaborate closely with emergency and social services
  • support family wellbeing through respectful partnerships

Social Policy & Australian Responses

Australian Statistics

  • Over 8 million Australians were impacted by the 2019–20 bushfires.
  • Floods have displaced tens of thousands of families in recent years.
  • Family violence reports increase significantly during crises (AIC, 2022).
  • Children in disaster-affected communities show higher trauma symptoms (AIHW, 2023).

Key Policies and Frameworks

  • National Disaster Risk Reduction Framework (Australia)
    Supports community preparedness and resilience.
  • Emergency Management Victoria / SES
    Guidelines for emergency procedures, evacuations, and safety.
  • MARAM Framework
    Guides safe responses to family violence crises.
  • Child Safe Standards
    Protect children from physical and emotional harm.
  • National Mental Health and Wellbeing Pandemic Response Plan
    Supports children's mental health in community crises.
  • ACECQA Regulations – Emergency & Evacuation Procedures
    All early childhood services must have evacuation plans, drills, and risk assessments.

Impact of Policy on Practice

Policies ensure that services:

  • conduct regular evacuation drills
  • maintain risk management plans
  • uphold safety and wellbeing standards
  • follow mandatory reporting requirements
  • collaborate with emergency services
  • support trauma recovery through wellbeing programs

Strategies for Practice

Five Evidence-Based Teaching Strategies

Strategy 1: Trauma-Informed Emergency Response
  • Use a calm voice and slow transitions.
  • Prioritise safety and predictability.
  • Provide reassurance during drills and emergencies.
Strategy 2: Restore Routines Quickly After Crisis
  • Keep the daily schedule consistent.
  • Use visual timetables.
  • Allow flexibility for children who need more comfort.
Strategy 3: Emotional Literacy & Safe Expression

Use puppets, drawing, stories, or sandplay to help children express feelings. Name emotions gently ("It's okay to feel scared"). Provide quiet spaces.

Strategy 4: Build Family Partnerships
  • Communicate sensitively and respectfully.
  • Share updates about their child's wellbeing.
  • Offer resources and referrals without judgement.
Strategy 5: Teach Safety & Preparedness in Age-Appropriate Ways

Simple fire safety lessons, stories about helpers (firefighters, SES, doctors), role-play and pretend play using emergency kits. Children learn empowerment rather than fear.
